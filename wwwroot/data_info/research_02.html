Introduction to and selecting Research Methods (Part 2)
  • Types of Question:
    • Open questions:
      • The question is asked in such a manner that the respondent can answer in any way he/she chooses. E.g.Describe the events that led up to the cancellation of the XYZ project?
    • Closed questions:
      • The question is asked in such a way that the respondent can only choose from a selection of pre-determined answers,. E.g. How frequently do you use the ABC information system?
        • More than once a week
        • 2 to 3 times a month
        • Once a month
        • Once a week
Advantages and Disadvantages of Open & Closed Questions
  • Advantages of open questions:
    • Respondents can answer in their own terms - Not forced to answer in the terms provided by closed answers.
    • Allow unusual responses to be derived - Replies that the survey researcher may not have considered (and that would therefore not form the basis for fixed-choice alternatives) are possible.
    • Do not suggest certain kinds of answer to respondents - Respondents' levels of knowledge and understanding of issues can be tapped. The salience of issues for respondents can also be explored. (for example: doctors/nurses/… and their responses)
    • Useful for exploring new areas or ones in which the researcher has limited knowledge - Exploratory and pilot studies to prepare later fixed-choice format answers
  • Disadvantages of open questions:
    • Time-consuming for interviewers to administer - Interviewees are likely to talk for longer than is usually the case with a comparable closed question.
    • Answers have to be ‘coded‘ - Very time-consuming and ambiguous a problem of meaning
    • Require greater effort from respondents - In case of a self-completion questionnaire, need to write for much longer.
    • Possibility in structured interviews of variability between interviewers in the recording of answers - Likely to arise as a result of the difficulty of writing down verbatim what respondents say to interviewers à problem of meaning
  • Advantages of closed questions:
    • It is easy to process answers - Codes emerge naturally.
    • Enhance the comparability of answers - Easier to show the relationship between variables and to make comparisons between respondents or types of respondents
    • Clarify the meaning of a question for respondents - Choice of answers may help to clarify the meaning of the concept
    • Easy for interviewers and/or respondents to complete - No need to write extensively
    • In interviews, closed questions reduce the possibility of variability in the recording of answers
  • Disadvantages of closed questions:
    • Loss of naturalness in answers - Respondent’s replies may not be covered by fixed answers provided
    • There may be variation among respondents in the interpretation of forced-choice answers - The problem of meaning
    • Closed questions may be irritating to respondents - Especially if they cannot find a category that they feel applies to them
    • In interviews, a large number of closed questions may make it difficult to establish rapport - The respondent and interviewer are less likely to engage with each other in a conversation, giving the interview an impersonal feel
Interviews:
  • An Interview is a special type of interaction between individuals where the purpose is for the person(s) undertaking the interview to obtain information from the other(s)
  • An interview is usually guided by an agenda or protocol and is not a random conversation
  • The interviewer generally asks the questions while the interviewee provides responses
  • All information given in the interview is “on the record” and is assumed to be recorded in some manner
  • When should we use Interviews?
    • To obtain detailed information
    • To ask complex, open-ended questions
    • To ask targeted, specific questions for different groups
    • To explore emotions, experiences, feelings not easily obtained otherwise
    • To investigate sensitive issues or informationTo obtain detailed information
  • What kind of questions to ask in the interview:
    • Introducing questions: `Please tell me about when your interest in X first began?'; `Have you ever . . .?'; `Why did you go to . . .?' .
    • Follow-up questions: getting the interviewee to elaborate his/her answer, such as `Could you say some more about that?'; `What do you mean by that . . .?'; ‘Can you give me an example…?’
    • Specifying questions: `What did you do then?'; `How did X react to what you said?’
    • Direct questions: `Do you find it easy to keep smiling when serving customers?'; `Are you happy with the amount of on-the-job training you have received?' Such questions are perhaps best left until towards the end of the interview, in order not to influence the direction of the interview too much.
    • Probing questions: following up what has been said through direct questioning.
  • Interview pitfalls (DANGERS):
    • Lack of time
    • Bias
    • Ambiguity of language
    • Confrontation, offence, and other personal issues
Questionnaires (closed):
  • In general, a questionnaire is a research instrument consisting of a sequence of pre-determined questions in a specific order
  • The responses to the questions are used in further analysis and interpretation
  • Questionnaires form the basis of most cross-sectional research designs, e.g. the survey
  • Types of questionnaires:
  • Self-administered: the respondent answers the survey on their own e.g.
    • Postal surveys
    • Internet surveys
    • Population census
  • Researcher-administered: the researcher guides the interviewee through the questions – equivalent to structured interviews
  • Self Administered vs. Researcher Administered (Advantages):
    • Cheaper to administer
    • Quicker to administer
    • Absence of interviewer effects
    • No interviewer variability
    • Convenient for respondents
  • Self Administered vs. Researcher Administered (Disadvantages):
    • Cannot probe (explore, look into)
    • Difficulty of asking many open-ended questions
    • Difficulty of asking complex or filtered questions
    • Questionnaire can be read as a whole
    • Cannot collect additional data
  • Procedural issues:
    • Careful planning and design:
    • Scope, relevance, further analysis
    • Types of questions, data types
    • Format, length
    • Delivery
    • Dealing with non-response
    • Pilot testing
    • Validity and reliability
