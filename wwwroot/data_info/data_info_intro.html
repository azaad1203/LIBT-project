Introduction to Data and Information

What is (are) data?
  • BIS: “The record of an event or fact” (C&C, 2008)
  • DB systems: “Raw (unprocessed) facts that have some relevance to an individual or organisation” (Connolly et al., 2008)
  • Wikipedia: “...represents the qualitative or quantitative attributes of a variable or set of variables...the lowest level of abstraction from which information and knowledge are derived (http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data, 05/09/09)
  • So information is data that has been processed or re-structured in some way and data are Raw facts, observations or measurements.
What is the purpose of information?
  • Why is information important?
  • Enables us to make good decisions by reducing uncertainty in our knowledge.
  • Also, we need to make sure that information should be:
  • Reliable – accurate, not (knowingly) misleading
  • Valid – relevant to the information need
The type of information in organisations
  • Decision making can range from highly structured to unstructured:
  • Strategic planning Management control Operation control
    Unstructured Company reorganisation Personnel management Dealing with customer enquiries
    Semi-structured Introduction of new product Analysis of performance Short-term production scheduling
    Structured Financial structure planning Allocating budgets Stock reorder decisions
  • Generating information always has a cost:
    • Data collection
    • Processing
    • Storage
Research:
  • General definition:
    • Search for knowledge and an attempt to establish facts
  • Kinds of research:
    • Exploratory – defining the nature of an issue/problem
    • Constructive – finding a solution to a problem
    • Empirical - testing the solution to a problem
  • Data and information requirements vary according to:
    • Purpose of the research
    • Nature of the problem
    • Existing knowledge
  • Research methods:
    • Many considerations are made when conducting research:
      • Primary vs. secondary research – does the knowledge already exist explicitly/implicitly somewhere in the world (e.g. the academic literature) or do we need to collect new data?
      • Laboratory vs. field studies – can we collect reliable data by studying the phenomenon ‘in the wild’ or do we need tighter control over the variable(s) of interest?
      • Cross-sectional vs. longitudinal – can the phenomenon be measured in a short session or is it necessary to measure it over an extended period?
How does data becomes information?
  • Data must be processed and structured to provide information.
  • Metadata provides an interface between user/system and data tables, fields (variables) and records (items):
    • Metadata - literally data about data, description of data
    • Enables the user to conceptualise and work with a set of data.
    • Describes the meaning (i.e. name), type, size and scope.
    • In SPSS (statistics application) each variable is defined by attributes including name, label, data type, category labels etc.
    • In a database the system catalogue or data dictionary holds metadata describing the contents and structure of the database.
